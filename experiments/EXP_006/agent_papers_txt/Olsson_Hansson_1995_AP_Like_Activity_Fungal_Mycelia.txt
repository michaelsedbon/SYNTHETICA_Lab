Radiation dose rates from patients receiving iodine-131 therapy 
for carcinoma of the thyroid

Abstract. Patients treated with radioiodine present a ra- 
diation hazard and precautions are necessary to limit the 
radiation dose to family members, nursing staff and 
members of the public. The precautions advised are usu- 
ally based on instantaneous dose rates or iodine reten- 
tion and do not take into account the time spent in close 
proximity with a patient. We have combined whole-body 
dose rate measurements taken from 86 thyroid cancer 
patients after radioiodine administration with published 
data on nursing and social contact times to calculate the 
cumulative dose that may be received by an individual in 
contact with a patient. These dose estimates have been 
used to calculate restrictions to patients' behaviour to 
limit received doses to less than 1 mSv. We have also 
measured urinary iodide excretion in 19 patients to esti- 
mate the potential risk from the discharge of radioiodide 
into the domestic drainage system. The dose rate decay 
was biexponential for patients receiving radioiodine to 
ablate the thyroid after surgery (the ablation group, A) 
and monoexponential for these receiving subsequent 
treatments for residual or recurrent disease (the follow- 
up group, FU). The faster clearance in the follow-up pa- 
tients generally resulted in less stringent restrictions than 
those advised for ablation patients. For typical activities 
of 1850 MBq for the ablation patients and 3700 MBq or 
7400 MBq for the follow-up patients, the following re- 
strictions were advised. Patients could travel in a private 
car for up to 8 h on the day of treatment (for an adminis- 
tered activity of 1850 MBq in group A) or 4 and 2 h (for 
activities of 3700 or 7400 MBq in group FU) respective- 
ly. Patients should remain off work for 3 days (1850 
MBq/group A) or 2 days (up to 7400 MBq/group FU). 
Partners should avoid close contact and sleep apart for 
16 days (1850 MBq/group A) or 4-5 days (3700 or 7400 
MBq/group FU). Contact with children should be re- 
stricted according to their age, ranging from 16 days 
(1850 MBq/group A) or 4-5 days (3700 or 7400 MBq in 
group FU) for younger children, down to 10 days (1850 
MBq/group A) or 4 days (up to 7400 MBq/group FU) 
for older children. The cumulative dose to nursing staff 
Correspondence to: S.F. Barrington for the week after treatment was dependent on patient 
mobility and was estimated at 0.08 mSv for a self-caring 
patient to 6.3 mSv for a totally helpless patient (1840 
MBq/group A). Corresponding doses to nurses looking 
after patients in group FU were 0.18-12.3 mSv (3700 
MBq) or 0.36-24.6 mSv (7400 MBq). Sensible guide- 
lines can be derived to limit the dose received by mem- 
bers of the public and staff who may come into contact 
with cancer patient treated with radioiodine to less than 
1 mSv. The rapid clearance of radioiodine in patients 
treated on one or more than one occasion means that 
therapy could be administered at home to selected pa- 
tients with suitable domestic circumstances. In most 
cases the restriction times, despite the high administered 
activities, are less than those for patients treated for thy- 
rotoxicosis. The concentration of radioiodide in domes- 
tic drainage systems should not pose a significant risk.

Introduction 
The greatest potential radiation hazard encountered in 
the practice of nuclear medicine arises from the adminis- 
tration of radioiodine; therapeutic doses of iodine-131 
commonly range from 150 to 7400 MBq, the larger ac- 
tivities being used to ablate thyroid remnants or to treat 
metastases in patients with thyroid cancer. Precautions 
are advised to limit the radiation exposure of members 
of the public and staff with whom a treated patient may 
come into contact. These precautions vary between 
countries, but recommendations are usually based on 
measurement of iodine retention or instantaneous dose 
rates [1, 2]. Treatment at home has been advocated [3], 
but the majority of patients who receive high doses of ra- 
dioiodine are isolated in hospital for a period of time for 
radiation protection purposes.

Potential sources of risk include both emitted radia- 
tion and excretion of radioactivity in exhaled breath, per- 
spiration, urine and saliva [4, 5]. Apart from urinary io- 
dide excretion other contamination risks are small com- 
pared with emitted radiation [6]. Direct irradiation of the 
public and health workers is the major concern. The In- 
ternational Commission on Radiological Protection 
(ICRP) recently revised their recommendations to re- 
duce the annual dose a member of the public should be 
allowed to receive from 5 mSv to 1 mSv [7]. To concur 
with these recommendations, changes in national guide- 
lines are well as the introduction of European-wide leg- 
islation are anticipated. 
The dose received by an individual from a patient will 
be governed by iodine distribution, the rate of iodine 
clearance and the time spent in close proximity with the 
person. Guidelines to limit radiation exposure should 
ideally make use, therefore, of iodine retention and in- 
stantaneous dose rate measurements in combination with 
published data on social and nursing contact times 
[8-11]. This paper sets out the restrictions that could be 
applied to patients with thyroid cancer using this ap- 
proach and also assesses the potential problems associat- 
ed with the discharge of radioactive urine into the drain- 
age system. 
Materials and methods 
Whole-body dose rates were measured in consecutive patients re- 
ceiving radioiodine for thyroid carcinoma. The study population 
comprised two groups, the ablation group (A) and the follow-up 
group (FU). The former included patients receiving radioiodine 
for the first time, to ablate the thyroid after surgery; the latter were 
patients receiving second or subsequent therapeutic doses for sus- 
pected residual, recurrent or metastatic disease. The activity ad- 
ministered to group A was dependent on the nature of thyroid sur- 
gery performed (hemithyroidectomy or "total" thyroidectomy). 
Activity administered to group FU was determined by the extent 
of disease, assessed by clinical examination, thallium scan, post 
therapy iodine scanning, plasma thyroglobulin levels, radiology or 
any combination of these. Patients were maintained on thyroxine 
and changed to triiodothyronine prior to therapy and this was dis- 
continued 10 days prior to the radioiodine administration and re- 
started after 7 days. 
Dose rates were measured on all patients at 1 m from the ante- 
rior mid trunk at intervals during admission and after hospital dis- 
charge for up to 11 days. Measurements at 0.1 m and 0.5 m were 
also made in some patients. Two dose meters were used, an RD8 
Universal Survey Meter (Wallac, Turku, Finland) and an RO10 
ionisation chamber (Eberline Instrument Company, Sussex, En- 
gland). The readings obtained from the two meters were normali- 
sed to the RO10 which was calibrated against a secondary stan- 
dard instrument using a caesium- 137 source. 
The dose rate was plotted against time and regression analysis 
using the method of least squares to fit the data was performed to 
derive the relationship between the two variables. The effective 
half-time for dose rate decay was then calculated for the two 
groups of patients. The following assumptions were made from 
previously published work with regard to contact between a treat- 
ed patient and members of the public [8, 9] and a treated patient 
and nursing staff [10, 11]: 1. For travel by private transport, the driver sat 1.0 m from the pa- 
tient and no other passenger was present in the vehicle. 
2. For travel by public transport, the patient was at a distance of 
0.1 m from fellow passengers. 
3. On return to work, the patient sat next to the same colleague at 
a distance of 1.0 m for a working day of 8 h. 
4. At home, the patient spent 6 h at 1.0 m, followed by 8 h at 0.1 
m (asleep) from the partner. 
5. The periods of time spent by a child in close contact with a pa- 
tient were assumed to be governed by the age of the child and 
were considered in three age groups: (a) the young infant (<2 
years) spent 15 periods of close contact (0.1 m) of 35 min during 
every 24-h period with the parent; (b) the 2- to 5-year-old child 
spent 4 h at 0.1 m from the parent and 8 h at 1.0 m; (c) the school- 
child aged 5-11 years spent 2 h at 0.1 m and 4 h at 1 m. 
6. For nursing contact, it was assumed that the nurse provided 
continuous care for an individual patient, working a 7-day shift 
for the first week after the patient was treated. The time spent and 
the proximity of contact with the patient varies according to the 
patient's dependency and was considered in five groups. The time 
spent per 8-h shift at 0.1 m, 0.5 m and 1.0 m from the patient was 
taken as follows: (a) with a totally helpless patient, 67 min, 128 
rain and 30 min, respectively; (b) with a partially helpless patient, 
17, 91, and 56 min; (c) with a chairfast/bedfast patient, 5, 56 and 
32 min; (a) with a semi-ambulant patient, 0, 7 and 31 rain; (e) 
with a totally ambulant patient, 0, 5 and 8 rain. 
Details of how patients are categorised into the above groups 
are given in the Aberdeen formula [11] and has previously been 
used by Mountford et al. [10] in the assessment of dose rates from 
adult patients undergoing nuclear medicine investigations. 
The cumulative dose received by members of the public who 
may come into contact with a patient was estimated using Mount- 
ford's method [12]. The method assumes that an individual is in 
regular contact with a patient for the same amount of time and that 
the duration of time between contacts (t) is identical. The doses re- 
ceived during each contact period form a geometric series where 
the common ratio between successive terms is e -~t, where 9~ is the 
effective dose rate decay, As At is small in comparison to a year, 
the annual cumulative dose can be calculated by summing the geo- 
metric series to infinity, if there is a monoexponential dose rate de- 
cay. For a biexponential decay, the total dose is calculated by add- 
ing the contribution of the two monoexponential decay curves and 
weighting them appropriately. The cumulative dose received by 
nursing staff was calculated by summing the dose received during 
each contact period over the 7 days following treatment. 
Periods of restriction were calculated by an iterativc process to 
find the number of whole days necessary to limit contact between 
a patient and a member of the public or designated "cater" to en- 
sure that the individual does not receive a dose exceeding 1 mSv. 
The model assumes that the dose received at a distance greater 
than 1.0 m is negligible. 
For comparative purposes, data were also collected retrospec- 
tively from tracer studies performed on thyroid carcinoma patients 
at St. Thomas's Hospital, London using a shadow shield whole- 
body counter (constructed in house). The effective dose rate decay 
was calculated from whole-body retention measurements made on 
days 1, 2, 3, 6 and 7 after administration of tracer doses of iodine 
(131I). 
Twenty-four-hour urine collections were also obtained in a 
subset of patients for up to 5 days after radioiodine administra- 
tion, to measure urinary iodide excretion. A 10-ml aliquot of the 
known volume of urine collected over each 24-h period was 
counted in a standard isotope calibrator.

Results

period, 42 were ablation treatments (group A) and 44

had dose rate measurements at 1.0 m. Eight in group A 
and 17 in group FU also had measurements made at 0.5 125 
m and 0.1 m. Amongst the group FU, disease was local- 
ised to the neck in 33. Eleven had metastases: four in the 
mediastinum, five in lung and two in bone. Mean activi- 
ty administered to group A was 2646 MBq (range 1074- 
5769). Mean activity administered to group FU was 
5461 MBq (range 3753-7622).

Discussion 
Advice given to patients receiving radioiodine therapy is 
based on retained activity or instantaneous dose rate 
measurement and takes little or no account of patient be- 
haviour. The doses received by staff, members of the 
public and family will be influenced by the patients' re- 
tained activity and the length and proximity of contact 
with those individuals. 
The retained dose will vary depending on the amount 
of functioning thyroid tissue present and the rate of renal 
and bowel excretion. The reduction in activity (measured 
by dose rate) in the ablation patients was biexponential, 
probably due to rapid redistribution of radioiodine from 
the stomach into the thyroid. These patients were a het- 
erogeneous group who had undergone a variety of surgi- 
cal procedures. Many patients had a total thyroidectomy 
but some had a lobectomy only and when treated signifi- 
cant amounts of thyroid tissue remained. The data were 
skewed towards the higher dose rates at the 1 m distance 
in group A by two such patients who had hemi- 
thyroidectomies and evidence of sizeable thyroid rem- 
nants on post-therapy scans. There were differences in 
the curve fits obtained at the various distances in both 
groups. The variation is, however, small and lies within 
the error of the measurement itself. The dose rates were 
particularly difficult to measure 4-5 days after treatment 
as they were sufficiently low as to be at the limit of the 
sensitivity of the dosemeters used. This made accurate 
definition of the slow component of radioiodine clear- 
ance for the ablation group using dose rate decay mea- 
surements especially difficult. However, the comparative 
whole-body counting data from the tracer studies con- 
firms the difference in the clearance of radioiodine in ab- 
lation versus follow-up patients, with a biexponential 
pattern seen in the former and a monoexponential pat- 
tern in the latter. It is reassuring that the half-time of the 
first component for group A and that of the single com- 
ponent for group FU from the whole-body tracer studies 
are very similar to those obtained from the dose rate de- 
cay measurements recorded from our therapy patients. 
The second component in group A from the whole-body 
counting data was, however, significantly longer in the 
St. Thomas' whole-body data. This would seem to add 
weight to the likelihood of inaccuracies occurring in de- 
fining the second component for the ablation therapy pa- 
tients. Other possible explanations for the discrepancy 
are the small size of the sample of the St. Thomas' abla- 
tion patients (n=16) or that there is a true difference in 
the handling of small and large administered activities of 
radioiodine [13, 14]. Whatever the explanation, the dif- ference between the two second component fits is small 
and will have little impact on the overall clearance of ra- 
dioiodine. 
In the FU group, the majority of whom had only min- 
imal functioning thyroid tissue, there was a monoexpo- 
nential and more rapid dose rate decay. These data were 
skewed towards the higher values by two patients with 
pulmonary metastases and one with residual disease in 
the neck which accumulated radioiodine. The positive 
skew was greater in the FU group than group A because 
of the very high iodine retention of these three patients. 
The effect of these three patients on cumulative dose is 
more marked in behaviour models, such as the nursing 
model, where the individual spends significant periods 
of contact at the 0.1 m and 0.5 m distances because the 
sample size for these distances was smaller than for the 
1.0 m distance. Interestingly though, other patients with 
metastases did not produce dose rate measurements 
greater than two standard deviations above the mean. We 
have not made a distinction, therefore, between advice 
offered to follow-up patients with local and metastatic 
disease, although if a patient is known to have excep- 
tionally high retention within the neck or within metas- 
tases, advice could be modified for that individual. 
The use of the 95th percentile rather than measures of 
variance around the mean was intended to take account 
of the non-parametric distribution of the data in both 
groups, allowing a high predictive confidence for the pa- 
tient population. The clear difference in the kinetics of 
therapeutic radioiodine between the two groups implies 
that the nature of the advice offered to patients will need 
to be altered for initial and subsequent treatments. It 
may also change depending on whether a single or sev- 
eral treatments are administered in 1 year. 
The periods of restriction calculated for group A cov- 
er a similar time period to those recommended for thyro- 
toxic patients in previous work from our institution [8]. 
The restrictions for group FU are, however, significantly 
shorter than for the thyrotoxic patients. Culver and 
Dworkin also observed this in their patients; they con- 
cluded that although the initial dose rates were higher in 
patients treated for carcinoma compared with thyrotoxic 
patients, the carcinoma patients cleared the iodine more 
rapidly. Contact with others could therefore be resumed 
more quickly. The 1-m dose rates in the study by Culver 
and Dworkin were very similar to our own, although di- 
rect comparison is difficult because their measurements

The length of time for which patients in group A 
treated with 1850 MBq (50 mCi) should be advised to 
sleep separately from their partner or to restrict contact 
with children under 2 years of age is 16 days. The dose 
to such a child if the advice is not heeded is 33 mSv and 
indicates that a period of isolation between child and the 
treated parent would be desirable. This may be achieved 
by the child staying with extended family for the 16-day 
period of restriction or, if this is not possible, then at the 
clinician's discretion the parent may be required to stay 
in hospital for a prolonged period. The advice given to 
patients on subsequent treatments (group FU) is likely to 
cause fewer problems with domestic arrangements as the 
corresponding periods of restriction are significantly 
shorter, at 4-5 days. The present UK guidance notes rec- 
ommend that close contact may be resumed with chil- 
dren of all ages once the retained activity in the patient 
falls to 30 MBq [1]. Using our data encompassing the 
social model and the biokinetic data the retained activity 
may vary between 29 and 230 MBq before the "normal" 
social contact can be resumed. This discrepancy between 
the advice given in the UK guidance notes and our own 
results calls into question the merit of advice based sole- 
ly on retained activity. 
The data with regard to doses incurred by nursing 
staff, if we consider an annual dose limit of 1 mSv, indi- 
cate that the administration of radioiodine to patients 
who are not at least semi-ambulant is impractical, unless 
nursing duties are shared amongst several individuals. 
Pregnant members of staff are precluded from nursing 
any individual who is not at least semi-ambulant and 
should be avoided if at all possible even if the patient is 
self-caring. 
The sharing of nursing duties amongst several indi- 
viduals may have important resource implications for a 
busy radiotherapy ward. Consideration should be given 
in the interpretation of these figures as to whether an in- 
dividual nurse will be required to care for more than one 
radio-iodine-treated patient in a single year. A nurse em- 
ployed in a radiotherapy ward may also be looking after 
other patients with radiation sources in situ and this will 
need to be taken into account. The figures are, of course, 
based on UK nursing practices and may need to be mod- 
ified if European working patterns are substantially dif- 
ferent. 
The ICRP has suggested that nurses and adults who 
knowingly and willingly incur exposure to radiation in 
the role of "carers" to patients undergoing therapy could 
be regarded separately fi'om members of the public [7]. 
These individuals would then be subject to dose con- 
straints rather than dose limits. Data in the form of cu- 
mulative dose estimates such as those presented here for 
both the home and hospital situation may be useful in the appropriate definition of dose constraints. The Na- 
tional Radiological Protection Board has suggested that 
a reference dose level of 5 mSv is appropriate in this cir- 
cumstance and would apply to each treatment, rather 
than function as a cumulative dose permissible in any 
one year [ 16]. 
The rapid clearance of radioiodine from patients re- 
ceiving follow-up treatments raises the possibility of 
treating selected patients with suitable domestic circum- 
stances at home. The potential doses received by nurses 
could be used as an argument for sharing the radiation 
burden with family carers in a domestic rather than a 
hospital environment. This would also have significant 
financial and resource implications. A suitably trained 
person could travel to the patient's home to administer 
radioiodine therapy. Alternatively the patient could be 
treated in hospital and allowed to drive home, preferably 
unaccompanied by their partner. As a prerequisite for 
home treatment, the clinician responsible for the pa- 
tient's care should be clear that the patient and adult 
family members can adequately comprehend the radia- 
tion risks attached to treatment and the necessary behav- 
ioural restrictions after appropriate counselling. Admis- 
sion to hospital for observation is usually desirable for 
first treatments, because of possible nausea and radiation 
thyroiditis. Thereafter, measurements presented here in- 
dicate that enforced hospital isolation, whilst occasional- 
ly indicated on clinical grounds, should not be mandato- 
ry for radiation protection purposes. Whilst significant 
exposure of family members can occur, the clinician in- 
volved could use his/her judgement to decide whether an 
individual patient may be treated at home, provided 
there are no children in the family. The concept of adult 
family members acting as "carers" should also be in- 
cluded in the decision-making process. 
Other considerations for home treatment are contami- 
nation within the household and outside the household 
due to excreted activity via sewage. The risk within the 
household from contamination via perspiration, saliva 
and exhaled breath should be considered in addition to 
that from emitted radiation. For carcinoma patients, the 
contamination risk is highest at 24 h. In comparison with 
emitted radiation though, the risk is small [4, 5]. Radio- 
iodine transferred through contamination to family 
members is almost entirely concentrated in the thyroid. 
In one study where skin and thyroid doses were mea- 
sured, external exposures exceeded internal thyroid dose 
equivalents by a factor of 113 [6]. Contamination can be 
substantially reduced by simple hygiene precautions in- 
cluding frequent hand washing and showering, avoiding 
sharing eating utensils or toothbrushes and the wearing 
of disposable slippers to prevent the transfer of sweat via 
floor surfaces [4, 6]. 
The outside contamination risk which is related to ex- 
cretion of iodine into domestic water drainage systems 
does not appear to pose a problem for sewerage employ- 
ees. The activity concentrations in the effluent is higher 
than recommended by the Association of University Ra-

Our data show that sensible guidelines can be drawn 
up to restrict the annual dose to less than 1 mSv for chil- 
dren of all ages and members of the public. This dose 
limit can also be achieved for partners and for nurses but 
these groups are likely to be regarded as "carers" and to 
be able to receive higher doses. The data presented here 
could be used to determine suitable dose constraints for 
these carers. The restrictions can be modified according 
to the number of therapies administered in 1 year. It is 
clear that measurements of retained activity, without ref- 
erence to patient behaviour patterns, are insufficient to 
formulate adequate advice on restrictions.
